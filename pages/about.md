# About the JAAZ Oral History Project 

 The *Japanese Americans in Arizona (JAAZ) Oral History Project* began in 2003 at Arizona State University when Dr. Karen J. Leong (Women and Gender Studies) and JACL AZ President Ted Namba received an AZ Humanities Programming grant for this project in 2002, with additional support from JACL AZ members. The original committee collecting oral histories and exploring state and city archives for additional information included JACL members Doris Asano, Donna Cheung, Marilyn Tang, and Kathy Inoshita. Members of families who settled in Arizona prior to World War II were initially interviewed. The premise of the project was that JACL AZ members would interview the the Nisei about their family histories coming to Arizona.

A February 2003 symposium held at Mercado Center about oral history featured scholars Dr. Valerie Matsumoto (University of California, Los Angeles) and Dr. Andrew B. Russell (Central Community College of New Mexico) explained the value of  oral history to document the history of Japanese Americans in Arizona before and during World War II. Matsumoto was among the first to document how the Japanese American community in Maricopa County was divided by the military zones set up by U.S. military command. Her ASU senior honors thesis still remains the best source about the experiences of first-generation (Issei) Japanese immigrant women settling in Arizona.  Russell  wrote his dissertation about Japanese Americans wartime experiences in Arizona, documenting the experiences of Japanese American railroad workers in Winslow Arizona who were detained by their employer, the Southern Pacific Railroad, and only survived because Navajo Indian co-workers brought them food. 

Community members received hands-on oral history training from Dr. Mary Logan Rothschild (Arizona State University)and Dr. Arthur Hansen (Director, Center for Oral History, California State University, Fullerton). Most of the oral history collection for this project took place between 2003 and 2011, collected by Leong and Asano. Community member Nikki Hara transcribed many of the original oral histories and Doris Asano was key in persuading many of the participants to share their family histories.

In 2005, the Japanese American National Museum received an Institute of Museum and Library Services Grant to document and create five distinct state-based curriculums about the wartime incarceration experiences of Japanese Ameriaans in Arizona, New Mexico, Colorado, Utah, and Texas--states whose communities often had been overlooked in the historical record to that time. Dr. Leong led the Arizona effort, which recruited five educators from across the state to participate in creating curriculum for grades 4, 8, and 12 based on oral histories collected about the war time experiences of Japanese American individuals and family members. This second project, *Enduring Communities Arizona* (ECAZ), added the oral histories of people's experiences during World War II. Because part of Arizona's Japanese American community did not reside within the designated military zones, some were not incarcerated yet still had unique wartime experiences to share. Additionally, some of the interviews included non-Japanese American individuals who provide their memories about the attitudes toward Japanese Americans in Arizona, including members of the Gila River Indian Community and the Colorado River Indian Tribes, upon whose reservations the two Arizona prison camps were located, the son of the second-in-command of Poston, and allies who defended Arizona Japanese Americans during World War II. 

Local high school students involved with the LEAD academy, a summer camp sponsored by ASU and the local AAPI community  about leadership that focused on Asian American and Pacific Islander history and experiences, collected several of the oral histories for this project, including interviews with JACL AZ members, the son of the assistant director of Poston Internment Camp, and a lawyer who shared memories of Simpson Cox, a local lawyer who defended Japanese Americans against unlawful loss of property during the war.  Dr. Christina M. Smith, then a graduate student in ASU Hugh Downs School of Communication, trained the students in videography, and also created the videos on this website about specific aspects of Arizona Japanese American experiences to be used by K-12 educators in the classroom. Most of the *Enduring Communities* interviews were collected between 2005 and 2008, and the funding provided by JANM allowed the hire of ASU undergraduate student workers to transcribe the oral histories.

The original website for this project was funded by a 2011 National Parks Service Confinement and Ethnicity Grant secured by Leong and Roisan Rubio, coordinator for Asian Pacific American Studies. The NPS allowed the Asian Pacific American Studies to hire several undergraduate students to develop a website that featured the oral histories. Under Rubio's supervsion, students created the site with a JAAZ and ECAZ featuring oral history excerpts to highlight childhood, family, and wartime experiences. The site also provided the 4th, 8th, and 12th grade lesson plans and videos developed for Arizona educators.  The site was unsustainable after ASU decided to no longer support Drupal after a few years, which is why this new site has been built. This site is an attempt to make the materials accessible again for educational use.

JAAZ therefore will include (at some point) all of these oral histories, although the JAAZ collection about community-building pre-World War II is separated out from the Enduring Communities materials within the archive itself for ease of navigation.

## About CollectionBuilder CSV
